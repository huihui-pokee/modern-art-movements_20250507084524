Introduction to Modern Art

Modern art movements mark a significant shift from traditional art styles, embracing abstraction and experimentation from the late 19th to the late 20th centuries. These movements have had a lasting impact on visual culture and continue to influence contemporary art.

Key Modern Art Movements

Abstract Art

This style does not attempt to represent external reality, but seeks to achieve its effect using shapes, forms, colors, and textures.

Art Deco

Flourished from about 1880 to 1910 as an international art movement and is a highly decorative style that celebrated natural forms.

Art Nouveau

This style is characterized by its elegant, decorative designs that often include intricate patterns and flowing curves.

Cubism

Led by Pablo Picasso and Georges Braque, Cubism breaks objects into geometric forms and depicts them from multiple perspectives simultaneously. It paved the way for abstract art.

Key Artists

  • Pablo Picasso
  • Georges Braque
  • Robert Delaunay
  • Marcel Duchamp
  • Juan Gris
  • Lyonel Feininger

De Stijl

This movement is characterized by the use of basic geometric forms and primary colors.

Expressionism

Originating in Germany, Expressionism conveys emotions and ideas rather than depicting the observable world.

Key Groups

  • Die Brucke
  • Der Blaue Reiter

Fauvism

Known for its bold and vivid colors, the term \"Fauvism\" comes from the French word \"fauve,\" meaning \"wild animals\".

Futurism

Celebrated modern technology, change, speed, and the machine age.

Key Artists

  • Umberto Boccioni
  • Carlo Carrà
  • Giacomo Balla
  • Natalia Goncharova

Impressionism

Impressionism originated in Paris as a reaction against formal and rigid painting styles and focuses on the effects of light on objects, often depicting landscapes and scenes of daily life.

Key Artists

  • Claude Monet
  • Pierre-Auguste Renoir
  • Alfred Sisley

Op Art

Movement that used abstract patterns with stark contrast to create different visual effects.

Photorealism

A genre of art that encompasses painting, drawing and other graphic media, in which an artist studies a photograph and then attempts to reproduce the image as realistically as possible in another medium.

Pop Art

Reacting against the elitism of abstract painting, Pop Art draws inspiration from consumer goods and comic imagery to reconnect art with everyday life.

Key Artists

  • Andy Warhol
  • Roy Lichtenstein
  • Jasper Johns
  • Robert Rauschenberg
  • David Hockney

Post-Impressionism

Emphasis on symbolic content and the artist's interpretation of the world. It shared characteristics with Impressionism, such as vivid colors and expressive brushwork, but focused on distorted forms and unnaturalistic colors to depict emotions.

Key Artists

  • Paul Cézanne
  • Vincent Van Gogh
  • Paul Gauguin
  • Georges-Pierre Seurat
  • Paul Signac

Realism

An artistic movement that began in France in the 1850s, after the 1848 Revolution. Realists rejected Romanticism, which had dominated French literature and art since the early 19th century.

Surrealism

Known for its visceral and aesthetic images, Surrealism is remembered for its production.

Key Artist

  • Salvador Dalí

Suprematism

An art movement focused on basic geometric forms, such as circles, squares, lines, and rectangles, painted in a limited range of colors.

Vorticism

A British art movement that flourished in London in the early 20th century. The Vorticists published two issues of the literary magazine BLAST, in June 1914 and July 1915.
